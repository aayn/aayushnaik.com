On free will
Reading Strawson against the compatibilists
Importance7 / 10
Confidencelikely
Statusin progress
Stancewithholding

The compatibilist move1 feels like changing the subject. If we redefine "free" to mean "uncoerced," we get a notion that fits the world but leaves the original question — the one that animated the debate — entirely untouched.

Strawson2 presses harder. To be truly responsible, you would have to be the cause of your own character. But your character is the product of nature and nurture, neither of which you chose. The regress3 is unavoidable, and at the bottom there is nobody at home.

This argument is what makes me suspicious of compatibilism even in its most sophisticated forms…

¹Frankfurt-style cases sidestep the deeper issue rather than resolving it.
²Galen Strawson, "The Impossibility of Moral Responsibility" (1994).
³To choose your character you'd need a prior character that did the choosing — and so on, forever.
