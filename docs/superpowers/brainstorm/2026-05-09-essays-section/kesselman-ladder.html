Kesselman's verbal-probability ladder

Eight levels mapping verbal expressions to rough probability bands.

The ladder
Term · approximate probability range · band

Special tags · probability not applicable

log Recording without claim — pile of links, snippets, observations. Not arguing for anything.
emotional Felt, not endorsed. Writing entangled with complex feelings; not a probabilistic claim about the world.

Where it comes from

Sherman Kent (CIA, 1964) wrote Words of Estimative Probability after a 1951 intelligence estimate said Soviet attack on Yugoslavia was a "serious possibility" — which one reader took to mean ~20%, the writer had meant ~65%. The 60-point misreading prompted him to propose pinned verbal terms: almost certain · probable · chances about even · probably not · almost certainly not.

Rachel Kesselman (American Military University thesis, 2008) studied decades of intelligence reports and showed Kent's terms still drifted in actual usage. She proposed the eight-level vocabulary above as a tighter standard.

ICD 203 (the current US intelligence directive) uses a 7-step variant: Almost No Chance · Very Unlikely · Unlikely · Roughly Even Chance · Likely · Very Likely · Almost Certain with explicit probability ranges.

Gwern adopted Kesselman's eight terms and added two non-probability tags (log, emotional) for cases where "how likely" doesn't apply.

The empirical reason this exists at all: studies show people read "likely" anywhere from 25% to 90%; "probable" from 30% to 90%. Standardizing the vocabulary doesn't fix the spread completely, but shrinks it.
