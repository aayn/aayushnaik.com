Whether billionaires should exist is one of those questions that locks people into teams before they've actually thought about it. Most discussions perform positions instead of testing them. This is my attempt at the honest version.

I find myself arriving at *mostly agree* — it is okay for billionaires to exist, with stipulations, particularly around hereditary wealth. The reasoning takes some work to lay out, so I'm doing it argument by argument. Each main consideration gets a section: the case for, the case against, and where I personally land.

## Value creation, not extraction

Suppose you build a business that serves 100 million people, each of whom gets ~$100 of value from it. That's $10B of value created. The business is worth $40B factoring in growth. You own 10%. You're a billionaire.

What exactly went wrong here?

### For

The wealth comes from creating something that didn't exist before. The 100 million people who paid for the value got more than they paid (consumer surplus). The founder captured a fraction of the value they generated. The world is richer; the founder is rich; no one is poorer than they would have been if the company hadn't existed. Bezos didn't grow rich by taking from middle-class Americans — he built Amazon, which created enormous consumer surplus (faster delivery, lower prices, cloud infrastructure that runs much of the modern internet) and captured a fraction of it.

### Against

First, not all wealth is value-creation. Some fraction comes from wage suppression, supplier pressure, externalities (local-business destruction, environmental costs), and public-infrastructure capture (USPS subsidies, decades of tax-free internet sales). Second, "the founder captured a fraction" hides the fact that *which* fraction is a policy choice, not a law of nature. We could permit huge upside without permitting unlimited accumulation. Third, predistribution mechanisms — mandatory ESOPs, capped founder equity — leave the value-creation structure intact while distributing the captured surplus more broadly. Different capture rule; same value.

### Where I land

The basic case is right. Most billionaire wealth is downstream of real value creation, not extraction. But the framing slightly oversells: the rule that lets the founder keep most of the captured surplus is itself a policy choice, and a different rule could distribute the upside more broadly without unwinding the value. The case for *value creation* is strong; the case for the *current capture rule* is weaker.

## Pluralism: protection from state failure

States can go wrong. Sometimes catastrophically. Venezuela post-2000. Cuba post-1960. The United States after 9/11, when an entire surveillance apparatus was built up with very little public debate. A society where the only large concentrations of power are political concentrations is a society where, if the political process goes wrong, there's nothing else to push back.

### For

Diffuse power is safer than concentrated power — Madisonian intuition that survives most empirical tests. Billionaires are one form of diffuse power: there are thousands of them<Sidenote>Forbes 2026 lists 3,428 billionaires worth $20.1T combined across 80 countries. Different industries, different ideologies, different priorities — no single private actor commands all the off-state resources.</Sidenote>; they don't agree; they push in different directions. A media-mogul billionaire backs one party, a tech billionaire another, an industrial billionaire something else. Soros funds civil-rights litigation when courts won't. Gates delivers vaccine logistics faster than the WHO can. The exit space exists.

### Against

The empirical record on whether billionaires actually *check* state power going wrong is mixed at best. Iraq War: billionaires were divided, with Murdoch's News Corp the single biggest amplifier of war propaganda. Post-9/11 surveillance: tech billionaires were *complicit* — Google, Facebook, Apple, the telcos all cooperated with NSA programs. Trump-era democratic erosion: the billionaire class was split, with enabling at least as strong as resistance. Theoretical pluralism is one thing; practical pluralism is another. And the alternative checks on state power (independent press, civil society, courts, NGOs) exist independently of billionaires — many of them, including the ACLU, are partly billionaire-funded but not constituted by billionaires.

### Where I land

The pluralism intuition is right; the empirical track record of *billionaires specifically* exercising it is weaker than the theory suggests. Diffuse power is a public good. Billionaires are one source of it. They're not the only source, and not particularly reliable, but their existence does provide some hedge against state runaway.

## The exit option: overcoming institutional paralysis

In any society, consequential decisions get made by *someone*. The candidates: political processes (subject to capture, short-termism, populist distortion), corporate boards (subject to quarterly pressure and fiduciary risk-aversion), or founders/billionaires (subject to their own judgment, with personal long-term consequences). Each has pathologies. But concentrated private capital is the only category that can override the other two when they get stuck.

### For

SpaceX exists because Musk could overrule both NASA's risk-averse politics and Boeing's quarterly capitalism. Bill Gates delivered global vaccine distribution faster than the WHO could politically. Bloomberg's tobacco-control work moves faster than any treaty body. The billionaire's freedom to act is itself a public good when democratic and corporate processes are too slow or too captured to deliver something that needs delivering.

### Against

The exit option is selectively cited. We notice the wins; we don't count the losses — when a billionaire-funded initiative degrades public discourse, captures regulators, or cements monopolies. SpaceX also worked *with* the political process (NASA contracts, FAA approvals, decades of public R&D in rocketry); the framing "billionaire alone overrode politics" is post-hoc and incomplete. And institutional paralysis is sometimes a feature, not a bug — slowness in political processes reflects democratic deliberation. "Rapid private decision-making" is a value only when the decision-maker has good judgment, which the market doesn't screen for.

### Where I land

Real but partial. The exit option enables some genuine wins — vaccine logistics, frontier tech bets — that wouldn't have happened in pure political or pure shareholder-capitalism contexts. But it's not a clean win: it requires the billionaire's judgment to be good, and the market doesn't directly select for that. Net: a modest plus, not a clean argument.

## A different path to influence (not via politicking)

The political path to power selects for a particular personality. To win elections, you have to be willing to do propaganda, master the swing-voter dance, be Machiavellian in dozens of small ways. The path to building a billion-dollar enterprise selects for different traits: vision, technical insight, risk tolerance, patience. Bill Gates and Jensen Huang would be terrible Senate candidates and probably wouldn't try. Building gave them a different ladder.

### For

Diversity at the top matters for the same reason diversity in any decision-making body matters — different selection mechanisms surface different competences. A political class that includes only people good at politicking is missing the perspectives of people good at building. The market path, with its flaws, gives those perspectives a way up. Quieter, more technical types — Sutskever, Page, Huang — who would never thrive in elective politics get influence without having to compromise into a politician's shape.

### Against

"Different selection mechanism" doesn't necessarily mean "better selection mechanism." The market selects for ability to outcompete in markets, which correlates with ruthlessness and willingness to externalize costs. The Microsoft path that produced Bill Gates wasn't quiet engineering excellence — it was years of antitrust violations, predatory bundling, and FUD campaigns against competitors. The "thoughtful Gates" image is largely a post-2000 philanthropic rebrand. Plus: most billionaires aren't Gates. The modal billionaire is a hedge fund manager, real estate developer, or commodities trader<Sidenote>Forbes 2025 by sector: Finance & Investments leads with 464 billionaires; Tech is second with 401. The "quiet builder" archetype applies to a small unrepresentative slice — maybe 5–10% of the class.</Sidenote>. The "different and worth having" path is unrepresentative.

### Where I land

The principle (we want non-political paths to influence for non-political personalities) is right. Whether *billionaires as a class* are a good vehicle for it is more contested. The strongest version is "a few rare cases prove the model works." The weakest version is "modal billionaire ≠ different-personality archetype." Both are partly true. The principle survives; the broad claim doesn't.

## Innovation: asymmetric bets and the civilizational record

Most consequential innovation is high-probability-of-failure, low-probability-of-massive-payoff. SpaceX nearly went bankrupt three times. Amazon ran losses for seven years. Moderna's mRNA platform was decades of dismissed research. The system that produces billionaires has also produced — since 1990 — the digital revolution, the biotech revolution, smartphones, search engines, modern medicine, AI candidates. ~1.3 billion people left extreme poverty.

### For

Public funding handles foundational research; commercial moonshots need someone willing to bet personal capital and corporate existence. Public companies can't do this — quarterly earnings and fiduciary risk-aversion. Only concentrated private capital can absorb 90% failure rates while waiting for the 10x payoff. Without billionaires willing to take these bets and bear the personal consequences of failure, many technologies we now consider essential would have arrived decades later or not at all. The empirical record on civilizational advance under billionaire-producing systems is unambiguous.

### Against

The causal attribution is confounded. Systems that produce billionaires also have democracy, rule of law, mass education, public R&D, immigration, and global capital flows. Attributing the gains to *billionaires specifically* requires holding all those constant — and they aren't. Three specific points: First, most VC capital comes from pension funds, university endowments, and sovereign wealth — *institutional* capital, not billionaire wealth. The asymmetric-bet capacity exists in pooled patient capital. Second, the 1.3B poverty exit happened largely in *China* (which suppressed billionaire wealth-power for most of the period) and *India* (state-led development); the number doesn't actually credit billionaire-ecosystem countries cleanly. Third, per-capita innovation rankings are dominated by Switzerland and Sweden<Sidenote>WIPO Global Innovation Index 2025: Switzerland #1 (15th consecutive year), Sweden #2, USA #3. Three Nordic countries (Sweden, Finland, Denmark) in the top 10. The "billionaire concentration → more innovation" story isn't supported by the rankings.</Sidenote> — countries not particularly known for US-style billionaire concentration.

### Where I land

This is the strongest *for* argument and also the most confounded. Yes, the system has produced massive innovation. No, it's not clear that *billionaires specifically* are the causal mechanism — public R&D, institutional capital, integrated markets, and democratic stability also do enormous lifting. The honest version: concentrated private capital is *one* enabler of moonshot bets, not the only one. The civilizational record is real but doesn't isolate the billionaire variable.

## Concentration is inevitable; alternatives have grim track records

Wealth and power concentrate somewhere. The historical record on alternatives is grim. The Soviet Union confiscated essentially all private wealth → gulags + Holodomor + collapse → top 1% income share went directly to oligarchy (5% → 25%). Venezuela nationalized hundreds of businesses → food production fell 75%, hyperinflation, 7.7M emigrants, worst peacetime economic collapse in modern Western Hemisphere history. The UK 1970s had concentrated union power → Winter of Discontent → Thatcher → power moved back to capital.

### For

The pattern is clear: when you destroy a concentration without rebuilding accountable institutions, the wealth and power don't dissolve. They relocate, often worse. Compared to Soviet state ownership or Venezuelan party control, a society where private fortunes form the largest off-state concentration looks pretty good. Most billionaires are inert in the political sense — they hold the wealth, sometimes do philanthropy, mostly don't try to remake society. The cost of allowing them to exist is real but bounded; the cost of trying to prevent them has historically been catastrophic. *Allowing* concentration in the least-bad form may be wiser than trying to engineer it away.

### Against

"Soviet vs. status quo" is a false binary. The Nordic countries have wealth taxes, strong welfare states, sovereign wealth funds, and democratic accountability — *and* their states haven't become Soviet. Norway has had a wealth tax since 1892. Switzerland tops global innovation rankings with high taxes and no major billionaire class. Distributed concentration with institutional checks is a third option that the "alternatives are catastrophic" framing ignores. Also, "concentration relocates" cuts both ways: if you cite Russian oligarchy as a relocation outcome, you're conceding that *forms of concentration* matter, not that all forms are equivalent. The structural critique points at *unaccountable private power*, not at "any concentration anywhere."

### Where I land

The catastrophe-of-radical-redistribution point is genuine and forceful. Soviet/Venezuelan/Cuban experiments had outcomes worse than what they replaced. But the false binary is real: a third option (Nordic-style distributed concentration with strong institutional accountability) has functioned for over a century in the cleanest cases, even if it doesn't fully generalize. The argument *"any attempt at constraint produces catastrophe"* is too strong; *"radical state seizure produces catastrophe"* is right.

## The moral hazard of dissolution

A position is only as good as its enforcement mechanism. "No human should have this much wealth" commits you to some apparatus capable of enforcing the cap — and that apparatus becomes itself a center of power, typically state-coercive.

### For

We've seen what state apparatuses powerful enough to seize private fortunes produce: Venezuela, Soviet collapse, pre-reform China. The cure creates the disease. The "billionaires shouldn't exist" position can't be implemented at scale without producing something worse than what it's trying to fix. *Even if you grant the structural critique,* you have to weigh it against the structural risks of the enforcement mechanism. That weighing usually favors restraint.

### Against

This is a slippery-slope argument disguised as an empirical one. The choice isn't "no enforcement" vs. "Soviet-style seizure." We already have estate tax, income tax, antitrust, securities regulation, FTC, SEC. None have produced Venezuela. The question is *degree*, not *kind*. Norway's wealth tax has existed since 1892 — implementation is mundane bureaucratic enforcement. "Estate tax → Soviet" is the same fallacy as "income tax → Soviet": we've had aggressive taxation for over a century without sliding to authoritarianism.

### Where I land

The argument as stated overreaches. *Aggressive redistribution* sufficient to fully solve the structural problem (Sanders-style 70%+ wealth tax above $32B) hasn't been implemented anywhere successful, and where attempted at that scale has gone badly. But mild-to-moderate constraint (Nordic-style wealth taxes, robust estate tax, antitrust) doesn't have the catastrophic track record the steel-man implies. The slope isn't always slippery.

## Existing accountability is doing real work

A common move against the "let billionaires exist" position is to point at specific harms — Sacklers and the opioid crisis, Holmes and Theranos, Bankman-Fried and FTX, Murdoch and Fox News election lies. The argument: these people did real damage; the system that produced them is therefore bad. But each of these cases triggered substantial legal and social consequences.

### For

Bankman-Fried got 25 years. Holmes got 11. The Sacklers paid $7.4B and lost their pharmaceutical empire. Fox News paid $787M to Dominion. None of this is enough — harm exceeded punishment — but it's also not nothing. The system catches the most egregious cases. Public backlash matters: museums refused Sackler money; reputations were damaged; secondary social consequences accumulated. The accountability gap is partial, not total.

### Against

Existing accountability is calibrated for *fraud and clear crimes*, not for *legitimate political/cultural deployment of wealth*. Sacklers paid $7.4B but kept ~$11B for knowingly contributing to ~700,000 deaths — wildly disproportionate. Murdoch personally faced zero accountability for two decades of documented democratic erosion; the empire continues. Koch's 20+ years of climate-denial funding triggered no legal consequence. The pattern: existing accountability catches fraud, not wealth deployment. A senator who repeatedly lies to voters can lose elections. A surgeon who's incompetent loses their license. A billionaire who funds climate denial for two decades faces approximately none of these mechanisms.

### Where I land

Existing accountability does *some* real work and shouldn't be dismissed. It catches the worst frauds. But it's calibrated for fraud-detection, not for the structural concerns about fungible political power and indirect harm at scale. Strengthening it (campaign finance, lobbying disclosure, antitrust enforcement) is more productive than the global "abolish billionaires" framing.

## Hereditary wealth: the strong exception

The arguments above are about *first-generation* billionaires — people who built businesses and became wealthy in the process. The case for letting them exist is strongest there. The case for letting hereditary fortunes compound is much weaker.

### For (the position to defend)

I don't actually find a strong case for unlimited hereditary compounding. Most defenders of billionaires, when pressed, won't defend unbroken multi-generational dynastic wealth. The Walton family alone holds ~$400B across heirs of Sam Walton<Sidenote>The Mars family, the Koch heirs, the Hermès descendants, the Ambani succession follow the same pattern. Multi-generational compounding from a single fortune that none of the current holders earned.</Sidenote>; the structural problems of the original wealth are amplified, not reduced, when transmitted across generations.

### Against (the case for serious reform)

By any standard of desert, this is hereditary aristocracy. The "shirtsleeves to shirtsleeves in three generations" trope is empirically false at the top of the wealth distribution. The structural problems of unaccountable wealth (political influence, regulatory capture) are *amplified* when the wealth is inherited rather than earned — at least the founder once passed a market test. The heir hasn't even had that. The desert claim, the meritocratic claim, and the structural-pluralism claim all weaken when applied to inheritors instead of builders.

### Where I land

This is the strongest case against unconstrained dynastic wealth, and I agree with it. The right frame isn't *aggressive taxation* — it's *estate tax reform aimed at multi-generational compounding*. Most of the dodge currently happens through specific mechanisms: step-up in basis (heirs inherit appreciated assets at the new value, erasing lifetime capital gains), zeroed-out GRATs<Sidenote>Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts — a trust mechanic that lets ultra-wealthy families transfer appreciating assets to heirs at zero gift-tax cost. Originally narrow and legitimate; abused at scale by people with the resources to deploy it at full strength.</Sidenote>, and dynasty trusts in states that abolished the rule against perpetuities. Close those, restore a federal perpetuities limit, and the effective taxation of ultra-wealthy estates rises dramatically without changing the headline rate.

The principle: if you built it, keep most of it during your lifetime. If your great-grandfather built it, the system should not let that fortune compound across three generations untouched. This is the policy where I'd push hardest — not as confiscation, but as restoration of an architecture that prevents dynastic capture. Adam Smith opposed inherited aristocracy for free-market reasons; Andrew Carnegie argued the wealthy should give back; Theodore Roosevelt pushed for early estate taxes. The lineage isn't socialist — it's classical-liberal meritocracy taking itself seriously.

## Synthesis

Mostly agree, with stipulations.

The process by which most first-generation billionaires become billionaires isn't intrinsically wrong — they create real value, and capturing a fraction of it isn't theft from anyone. Their existence diffuses power away from a state apparatus that has historically been the worse failure mode. Forcing their non-existence by radical state seizure has produced catastrophe everywhere it's been tried. The system that produces billionaires has also produced rapid civilizational advance — though *how much* of that advance is causally due to billionaires (versus public R&D, institutional capital, education, rule of law) is more contested than the dominant narrative claims.

But: the structural concerns are real. Wealth at billionaire scale converts into fungible political and cultural power that escapes the accountability mechanisms constraining other forms of power. Hereditary compounding produces unearned aristocracy across generations. Some billionaires demonstrably cause civilization-scale harm (Murdoch, Sackler, Koch network, Russian oligarchs). These are not the median case — most billionaires are inert — but they're real, and they're not adequately addressed by existing accountability, which catches fraud rather than political deployment of wealth.

So: the stipulations.

- **Estate tax reform aimed at dynastic wealth.** Close the loopholes that allow fortunes to compound untaxed across generations: end step-up in basis on the largest estates, restore a federal limit on perpetual trusts, restrict zeroed-out GRATs. The principle isn't *punish wealth* — it's *don't let unearned multi-generational compounding produce hereditary aristocracy*. Loophole closure alone does most of the work; rate increases are secondary.
- **Progressive taxation that scales at very high wealth levels** — but not a hard cap. The marginal motivational value of dollars 5B–50B is approximately zero for personal consumption; tax it more steeply at the top. But capping risks killing serial entrepreneurship in a Pareto-distributed way (the rare next SpaceX-equivalent matters more than the headcount loss suggests).
- **Strong campaign finance and lobbying limits.** Sever the wealth-to-political-power conversion that's the actual structural worry.
- **Antitrust against clear monopolistic abuse** — predatory pricing, anti-competitive bundling, market-foreclosing acquisitions — not against size per se. The line is behavior, not scale.
- **Robust independent press and public institutions**, so society isn't dependent on billionaire largesse for the things philanthropy currently fills.

None of these require Soviet-style state seizure. All are politically achievable in democratic frameworks. Some are already implemented elsewhere.

What I'd dissolve isn't the founder of the next valuable business. It's the dynastic compounding three generations later. It's the wealth-laundered political influence. It's the unaccountable media empire that knowingly broadcasts election lies. It's the political infrastructure where a single donor's preference outweighs ten thousand voters'.

Those targets are achievable. *Allow billionaires to exist; constrain the structural pathologies* is the position I'd defend.

I hold this likely-but-not-certain. The Nordic generalizability question is genuinely open. The mechanism design is genuinely hard. I'd update on better evidence. But the central claim — that trying to engineer billionaires out of existence is mistaken, while constraining unaccountable concentrated power is wise — survives.
