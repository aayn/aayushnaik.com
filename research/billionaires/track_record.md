# Billionaire Track Record — Evidence Both Ways

Research notes for the essay *"Billionaires should not exist."* Goal: documented examples of billionaires causing measurable good and measurable harm, with magnitudes where available. Honest balance, not cherry-picking.

---

## I. Beneficial Cases (with documented impact)

### 1. Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation
- **Total spent (1995–2025):** ~$100B (60% from Gates, 40% from Buffett)
- **Annual budget (2025):** ~$9B; planned to rise to ~$10B/year through 2045 (sunset date)
- **2024 disease-specific spend:** Malaria $318M, TB $283M, HIV $216M
- **Documented health outcomes:**
  - Wild polio reduced from 350,000 cases/year (pre-foundation) to 12 cases in 2 countries
  - Malaria deaths cut in half over two decades
  - HIV infections down 60% since mid-1990s
  - Under-5 malaria deaths down >30% (2000–2019)
  - UN estimates 79M lives saved from TB efforts since 2000 (Global Fund partnership)
- **Verdict:** Among the most-evidenced cases of catalytic billionaire philanthropy. Lives saved measured in millions.

### 2. MacKenzie Scott (no-strings giving)
- **Total given (2019–2025):** $26.3B; ranks 3rd in lifetime giving (behind Buffett and Gates)
- **2025 alone:** $7.17B to ~225 organizations
- **Model:** Unrestricted donations — "adding value by giving up control." Median gift $5M. ~$1.06B to HBCUs.
- **Impact data:** 85% of recipients used funds to expand programming; 52% gained capacity to respond to community needs
- **Verdict:** Demonstrates an alternative model where billionaire money flows without strings, addressing the "billionaire-as-king-of-the-cause" critique.

### 3. Bloomberg Philanthropies (tobacco control)
- **Total tobacco-control spend (2005–2025):** ~$1.6B + $420M new commitment (2023)
- **Documented impact:**
  - 185 laws enacted across 94 countries
  - ~5 billion people now covered by at least one MPOWER tobacco-control policy (up from 1B in 2007)
  - Estimated 35.2M lives saved
- **Verdict:** Concrete public-health win at policy scale, replicable internationally.

### 4. George Soros / Open Society Foundations
- **Total spend (1993–2025):** $24.2B+ in expenditures; Soros has personally given $32B+
- **Model:** Civil society / pro-democracy / human rights advocacy worldwide
- **Caveat:** A 2022 academic study (GMU Schar) concluded "no proof that OSF funding has a substantial contribution at the macro-level in the development of open societies, nor that it is having the negative impacts that some of its critics ascribe to it." Translation: hard to measure either way at scale.
- **Notable:** Banned by Russia (2015), restricted in India (2016), Hungary, Turkey — i.e. authoritarian regimes treat OSF as a threat, which is itself evidence of perceived democratic effect.
- **Verdict:** Hard to measure. Real spending, contested impact.

### 5. Carnegie libraries (historical)
- **Total spend (~1880s–1919):** Carnegie gave ~$350M (~$10B in 2026 dollars)
- **Output:** 2,500+ public libraries worldwide; 1,700 in the US, ~100 at universities
- **Long-term impact:** Free public libraries became the American norm. Many still operate.
- **Mechanism design:** Funded buildings only if towns committed to ongoing public funding — embedded sustainability. Required free access for all.
- **Verdict:** Long-run institutional creation. Likely the strongest long-term case for catalytic private wealth.

### 6. Rockefeller Foundation (historical)
- **Built medical education infrastructure across the US** (early 1900s)
- **Funded schools of public health** that reshaped global disease control
- **University development program** that built modern research universities globally
- **Verdict:** Long-tail institutional impact still visible 100+ years later.

## II. Harmful Cases (with documented harm)

### 1. Sackler family / Purdue Pharma (opioid crisis)
- **Settlement (2025):** $7.4B — largest individual-responsibility opioid settlement
  - Sacklers pay $6.5B over 15 years; Purdue $900M
  - Forced exit from US opioid sales
- **Scale of harm:** Opioid crisis has killed ~700,000+ Americans since 1999 (CDC). Purdue's OxyContin was the catalyst. Internal Sackler emails showed they knew the addiction risks and pushed sales anyway.
- **Wealth impact:** Sacklers extracted ~$11B from Purdue before bankruptcy. Net family wealth still ~$11B+ post-settlement.
- **Verdict:** Direct, knowing, measurable mass harm for profit. Settlement is < harm caused.

### 2. Russian oligarchs / Putin kleptocracy
- **Pattern:** Roman Abramovich, Oleg Deripaska, and others built state-rivaling wealth via post-Soviet privatization, then served as enablers/launderers for Putin's regime
- **Mechanism:** "Provide mercenaries, key minerals for the war machine, launder money for the regime or for Putin himself"
- **Western complicity:** Oligarchs spent 20+ years laundering reputations through London/NYC real estate, soccer clubs (Abramovich → Chelsea), elite philanthropy. Sanctions only meaningfully applied post-2022 invasion of Ukraine.
- **Verdict:** Direct example of billionaire wealth enabling authoritarianism and aggressive war. The "billionaires as check on state" theory inverted.

### 3. Major fraud cases (Holmes, SBF, Neumann)
- **Theranos / Elizabeth Holmes:** Burned $700M in investor capital. Sold non-functional medical tech to patients. Sentenced 11+ years (2022)
- **FTX / Sam Bankman-Fried:** Funneled customer deposits to Alameda Research. Lost ~$8B in customer money + $2B investor money. 25 years federal prison (2024). Forfeited $11B
- **WeWork / Adam Neumann:** Self-dealing on building leases, trademark sale to own company, $700M cash-out before failed IPO. Wiped >$40B in paper value
- **Verdict:** Three cases of "billionaire" status produced by fraud at scale. Notable: each was venerated as a visionary by media before unraveling. Selection mechanism failed.

### 4. Koch network (climate denial funding)
- **Documented funding (1997–2017):** $127M to 92 organizations promoting climate denial
- **Single-period intensity:** Almost $25M from Kochs alone (2005–2008)
- **Evolution:** As traceable direct funding became politically costly, Kochs shifted to dark-money pass-throughs (Donors Trust, Donors Capital)
- **Documented mechanism:** Climate-skeptic think tanks, captive academic centers, "violations of academic integrity" via donor-influenced faculty appointments
- **Verdict:** Decades-long, well-documented, deliberate suppression of climate science to protect fossil-fuel profits. Counterfactual harm: how much earlier could climate policy have moved?

### 5. Rupert Murdoch / Fox News (democratic erosion)
- **2007 academic study (Stanford):** Introduction of Fox News on a cable system pushed local voters measurably to the right ("Fox News Effect")
- **2010 PIPA study (U. Maryland):** Fox News viewers more misinformed than viewers of any other major news source
- **Dominion lawsuit (2023):** Internal communications revealed Carlson, Hannity, Ingraham knew 2020 election fraud claims were false but broadcast them anyway, with Murdoch's explicit encouragement
- **Pattern:** Replicated across countries — Murdoch tabloids in UK (Brexit), Australia (climate policy), US (Trump-era election denial)
- **Verdict:** A single billionaire family demonstrably degrading the information environment of multiple democracies, over decades, with documented intent.

## III. Meta-Critique: Giridharadas / *Winners Take All* (2018)

Core arguments:
- Billionaire philanthropy "casts elites as part of the solution to problems they helped create" — converts unearned moral authority from wealth.
- Philanthropic agendas focus on what *doesn't threaten the donor's wealth* — education reform, technology for good — while structural problems (tax policy, labor power, inequality) get sidelined.
- "Pseudochange" displaces real democratic action. "There's enormous moral difference between five guys deciding to do something and a city deciding to do something."
- Better alternative: pay taxes; let democratic processes decide allocations.

This isn't an argument that philanthropy is harmful. It's an argument that *philanthropy as a substitute for taxation and democratic decision-making* is structurally distorting — even when the philanthropy itself is well-intentioned and effective.

---

## IV. Patterns Worth Examining

1. **Asymmetry of accountability**: When philanthropy works (Gates malaria), the win is real. When it goes wrong (Zuckerberg's Newark schools, gates of foreign-policy interference), there's no electoral consequence. Wins and losses both compound private power.

2. **"Good despite" vs. "good because"**: Gates Foundation's good work happened *despite* Microsoft's monopolist behavior, not because of it. Carnegie's libraries happened *despite* Homestead Strike, not because. Doesn't erase the good — but cuts against the "billionaires are net-positive because their philanthropy" frame.

3. **Selection bias in our discussion**: We hear about Gates and Sacklers. The ~3,000 *median* billionaires are mostly invisible — they're not particularly philanthropic, not particularly harmful, just rich. Most billionaire wealth sits compounding, doing nothing public-facing. The signature cases (good or bad) are unrepresentative.

4. **Direct harm is concentrated; direct good is too**: A small subset of billionaires do most of the documented good (Gates, Scott, Bloomberg, Soros, historical Carnegie/Rockefeller). A different small subset do most of the documented direct harm (Sacklers, Murdoch, Koch network, Russian oligarchs, the fraud cases). The bulk of billionaires fall in the middle — neither saving nor destroying lives, just holding wealth.

5. **Indirect harm scales differently than direct good**: Murdoch's polarization effect compounds across millions of viewers and decades. Koch climate denial may have delayed serious policy by 20+ years globally. The harm vectors operate at a structural scale that even Gates Foundation's $100B can't fully offset because they reshape the *system* the philanthropy operates within.

6. **No single billionaire has saved as many lives as any major democratic state's healthcare system**: For perspective — Gates Foundation saved millions; the UK's NHS treats ~60M people; even modest welfare-state interventions across G20 countries dwarf any individual philanthropy. This isn't to dismiss billionaire impact, but to scale it.
