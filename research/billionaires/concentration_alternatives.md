# Where Else Does Power/Wealth Concentrate?

Research notes for the essay *"Billionaires should not exist."* Examining the empirical track record of alternative concentration sites — to test the claim "billionaires might be the least harmful place for wealth/power to concentrate."

---

## I. Cases Where Non-Billionaire Concentration Went Badly

### 1. UK trade unions (1970s) — concentrated labor power
- Union membership peaked ~13.5M (1979) — more than 2× current levels
- 29.5M working days lost to strikes in 1979
- **Winter of Discontent (1978–79)**: hospital staff shortages, gravediggers struck, daily life collapsed
- Backlash → Thatcher elected May 1979 → series of employment acts (1980, 1982, 1984, 1988, 1990) gutted union power
- **Working days lost fell from 29.5M (1979) to 4.3M (1981)**, then continued falling
- Membership halved by century's end
- **Where the power went next**: not eliminated — *relocated to capital*. Thatcher-era deregulation enabled the financialization and tech-billionaire wealth concentration we see today. *The concentration didn't dissolve; it moved.*

### 2. Venezuela (Chávez/Maduro era) — state seizure of wealth
- Chávez nationalized hundreds of private businesses across aluminum, cement, gold, iron, steel, farming, transportation, electricity, food, banking, paper, media
- Oil profits redirected to social programs initially → real poverty reduction in early 2000s
- But: **food production dropped 75% over two decades** while population grew 33%
- Mid-2014 oil price collapse exposed the system; price controls, capital flight, brain drain accelerated
- **Hyperinflation began November 2017** (50% monthly), peaked >1,000,000% YoY
- **Per capita economic contraction: 61%** by end of decade
- **7.7M+ emigrants** (over 25% of pre-crisis population) — largest peacetime exodus in modern Latin American history
- Considered the **worst peacetime economic crisis in Western Hemisphere history**, more severe than US Great Depression
- **Where the power went**: state + ruling party + military, with *less* accountability than the oligarchy it replaced. Maduro maintains power through security forces, vote rigging, and selective brutality.

### 3. Soviet Union — total state confiscation
- 1928–1989: state owned essentially all productive wealth
- GDP per capita grew 5.2× over the period (genuinely impressive nominal economic growth)
- But: **gulag system** included millions of forced laborers; estimated 1.5–1.8M deaths from camps and dekulakization
- Famine in Ukraine (Holodomor, 1932–33): ~3.5–7.5M deaths
- Eventually: stagnation + 1986 oil price collapse → 1991 dissolution
- **Where the power went next**: *directly to oligarchy*. Privatization in the 1990s transferred state assets to insiders. Top 1% income share went from <5% (late Soviet) to **20–25% by 2015**. Top 10% share rose from <25% to >45%. Same wealth, new owners — and the new owners (Russian oligarchs) became Putin's enablers.

### 4. China — Party-state + princelings
- Wealth concentration via state-owned enterprises (SOEs) and Communist Party connections
- "Statist crony capitalism" — senior leaders' families control state-monopolized industries
- **Xi Jinping's family alone**: $1B+ in business investments and real estate (per US intelligence reports)
- 2014 ICIJ leak: 13 princeling families with offshore accounts, including Xi's brother-in-law
- **Anti-corruption campaign (2012–present)**: ~5M officials investigated, 4.7M found guilty — but campaign also serves to consolidate Xi's power against rivals (especially other princelings)
- Surveillance state: no political opposition, no free press, growing repression in Hong Kong/Xinjiang
- **Verdict**: real economic growth (lifted 800M out of poverty since 1980) but at enormous cost to political freedom; concentration is at the Party level rather than dispersed.

## II. Cases Where Distributed Concentration Worked

### 5. Nordics (Norway as exemplar) — distributed checks
- **Wealth tax**: 1% on net wealth $174K–$2M; 1.1% above; revenue 0.6% of GDP. Yes, some millionaires leave. System endures.
- **Sovereign wealth fund** (Norway Government Pension Fund Global): petroleum revenues invested globally, capped 3% annual withdrawal rate. Now ~$1.6T+ — largest in the world.
- **Strong unions** (~50% union density vs. ~10% US)
- **Free press**, robust democracy, civil society
- **No inheritance tax** since 2014 (interesting wrinkle — they tax accumulation, not transfer)
- **Outcome**: lowest inequality among rich countries (Gini ~0.27 vs. US 0.41), top-3 in nearly every quality-of-life ranking, top-10 economy by GDP per capita
- **Mechanism**: power distributed across *multiple* accountable institutions — strong state + strong unions + sovereign fund + free press + democracy. No single concentration dominates.

### 6. Singapore — technocracy + sovereign capital
- Two sovereign wealth funds: GIC + Temasek + CPF = **~$1.77T combined**
- Net Investment Returns Contribution funds education, R&D, healthcare, environment
- High growth, high standard of living, top-tier infrastructure
- **But**: low transparency (GIC doesn't disclose annual P&L), limited political opposition, restricted press freedom, defamation laws used to silence critics
- **Verdict**: economically successful but not democratic. A counter-example showing that *sovereign* concentration can produce good economic outcomes — at the cost of democracy. Trade-off, not free lunch.

## III. Patterns Worth Examining

1. **Concentration relocates rather than dissolves.** UK in the 70s: from unions back to capital. Soviet Union: from state directly to oligarchs. The naive "destroy concentration X" without rebuilding accountable institutions just shifts the problem.

2. **Radical state seizure has a catastrophic track record.** Venezuela, Soviet Union, Cuba, Maoist China — every attempt at "seize the wealth and redistribute via state command" produced famine, repression, brain drain, or all three. This is the strongest empirical case against "abolish billionaires" if read as "let the state take everything."

3. **But state seizure is not the only alternative.** The Nordics achieved low inequality + high quality of life through *distributed institutional design*: high taxes + strong unions + sovereign fund + free press + democracy. No single concentration dominates. The system endures across decades.

4. **The relevant axis isn't "private vs. state."** It's "*concentrated vs. distributed*" and "*accountable vs. unaccountable*." Soviet state and Russian oligarchy were both *concentrated and unaccountable* — they failed in different ways. Nordics are *distributed and accountable* — they work.

5. **Singapore complicates the picture.** Sovereign concentration *can* work if technocratic management is competent. But the cost is democratic accountability. The Nordic model is the only one delivering both equality and democracy.

6. **The "least bad concentration" framing is a false dichotomy.** It assumes wealth/power *must* concentrate in one of {billionaires, state, oligarchs, party, unions}. The Nordic case shows this isn't necessary — power can be distributed across many simultaneously-checked institutions.

---

## IV. The Steel-Man for "Billionaires as Least-Bad Concentration"

Granting maximum force to the argument:
- State takeover historically catastrophic
- Union dominance produces the British 70s
- One-party state produces China's surveillance dystopia or Venezuela's collapse
- Oligarchy produces Russia
- Most billionaires are inert; only some cause active harm
- Therefore: distributing wealth in private hands of mostly-inert billionaires beats the alternatives

The strongest version of this argument has real force *as a critique of radical state-seizure proposals*. It's a weak argument against the Nordic model.
